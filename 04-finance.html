Section 04 · Operating Model

Two revenue streams. One subscription.

The live site names three plans — Core, Growth, Performance — but doesn't price them. The sprint built the model that does: a fixed subscription fee (where our margin lives) plus a monthly credit pool the client spends on services from the ALC Menu (where our delivery partners earn 75% of cost). Different economics, different logic, one monthly invoice.

The shape

One invoice. Two economies inside it.

Every client plan splits into a fixed subscription fee (high-margin, covers GritFuel OS access + ops) and a credit pool (variable, 75% flows to delivery partners as service cost). Clients spend credits via the ALC Menu. Same invoice, two economies. Stream 1 underwrites the product. Stream 2 delivers the work. The next section breaks each one down on its own terms.

The model, on one page

Stream 1: the subscription. Stream 2: the credit pool.

The headline number the client sees is one monthly invoice. Inside that invoice, two different economies run side-by-side. Both are real revenue. Each has its own cost logic, margin profile, and partner destination.

STREAM 1 · FIXED SUBSCRIPTION FEE Access to the OS

What the client pays for: access to GritFuel OS (Compounding Engine, Dashboard, Escalation Router, Intake, Credit Allocation, Monthly Playbook), the operator time behind the rhythm, the ops overhead that makes the monthly cycle land on time.

Per tier / month:
Core €1,750 · Growth €2,500 · Performance €3,500

Margin profile: high. This is where the economics of the business live. Predictable MRR. Scales linearly with cohort count until we hit the capacity ceiling.

STREAM 2 · CREDIT POOL SPEND Services from the ALC Menu

What the client pays for: services — creative, paid media, performance, analytics, content, SEO, PR, social, email, events — drawn from the ALC Menu. 1 credit = €25 output value. Priced at ~70% of EU benchmarks.

Per tier / month:
Core €1,750 (70 credits) · Growth €2,750 (110 credits) · Performance €4,500 (180 credits)

Margin profile: thin by design. 75% of credit-pool cost flows to the delivery partners (.frank, Bluegyfu, BFL — all common ownership with GritFuel). 25% is our margin on services. Paid media runs at cost.

Why this framing matters

Calling it "plan + add-ons" collapses these two economies into something that reads like an agency retainer. It isn't. Stream 1 is subscription economics (SaaS-like margin on access). Stream 2 is a productised services marketplace (marketplace margin, partner cost share). The capital efficiency of the whole thing rests on keeping these separate in our heads, our quotes, and our books.

Headline the client sees

Core €3,500/mo · Growth €5,250/mo · Performance €8,000/mo. One price, one invoice, one monthly rhythm. The two-stream structure lives in the back-office. 3-month credit rollover.

How the artefacts connect

Cost → Capacity → Quote → Services catalog.

The Fixed-Cost Model sizes what Stream 1 must earn to sustain the business. The Headcount & Capacity Plan sets how many clients we can actually run at that margin. The Scoping Configurator turns an intake brief into a plan tier + a quote. The ALC Menu is the services catalog a client spends their credit pool on — it's where the Stream 2 economy happens, not a list of upsells.

Key decisions

What's locked, what's open.

Two-stream revenue model — locked

Fixed subscription fee (Stream 1) + credit pool (Stream 2) is the model. 75% cost-share on Stream 2 flowing to delivery partners is the deal. This is the foundation — everything else sits on it.

Locked

Core / Growth / Performance plan names — locked

Carried forward from the live site. Keeps continuity with anything founders have already seen. We change the mechanics inside, not the labels.

Locked

ALC Menu is the services catalog for the credit pool

Not an add-on menu, not edge cases — it is Stream 2's product surface. Every credit a client spends resolves to an ALC line item with a partner destination and a margin impact.

Locked

Public plan prices during beta — no headline numbers

Already decided. Core / Growth / Performance stay on the site without numbers for the beta cohort. Pricing continues to be discovered in the sales conversation. Revisit post-cohort-one once we have real anchoring data.

Locked

Surface the credit model publicly — to trigger curiosity without anchoring price?

Related but different question. We've agreed not to publish €-numbers in beta, but we could surface the credit mechanic itself — e.g. "Core: 70 credits/mo · Growth: 110 · Performance: 180. 1 credit = one unit of growth work." That's enough to signal productisation (not hourly, not retainer) and spark "how does that work?" questions on the sales call, without setting a price anchor.

Pro: differentiates instantly from agency retainers, makes the model feel systematic, qualifies-in founders who find that interesting. Con: people may try to back-solve to a €-number (70 × €25 = €1,750 = fixed, pool = fixed — a spreadsheet-native founder could get close). Mitigation: publish credits only, without the €25 unit economics — keep that for the sales conversation.

Proposal: publish credit counts per tier + a one-liner explaining what a credit does, but not the euro value of a credit. Your read?

Open

Do we expose the two-stream split to clients, or just the headline?

Showing the split makes our margin on Stream 1 transparent and the partner cost share on Stream 2 visible. Clean, defensible — but also negotiable. Showing only the headline keeps it simple and preserves pricing power.

Open

Performance tier — how much is outcome-based?

Options: (a) Stream 1 stays fixed, performance is a bonus on Stream 2 outcomes; (b) lower Stream 1 fee + rev share on client growth; (c) pure rev share. All three are supported by the model — the question is risk appetite.

Open

Extra credits mid-month — allowed, capped, or upgrade-only?

Question: what happens when a client burns through their pool before the cycle resets? Options: (a) no top-ups — wait or upgrade; (b) unlimited top-ups at standard €25/credit — simple but uncapped capacity exposure; (c) capped top-ups at flat rate — protects delivery capacity, forces upgrade conversations beyond the cap.

Proposal: option (c). Allow top-ups up to 25% of the plan's pool size per month at the standard €25/credit (no premium). Beyond that, the Escalation Router surfaces a plan-tier upgrade conversation instead of letting credits sprawl. No premium pricing on top-ups — we want clients asking for more work inside the OS, not penalising them for it. The discipline comes from the cap, not the price. Open question: is 25% the right ceiling, or do we want a tighter hard cap (e.g. +20 credits regardless of tier) so Performance clients can't quietly absorb +45 credits a month without a capacity conversation?

Open

€750 "Compound Diagnosis" — paid entry-point below Core?

Question: do we offer a paid, short-form diagnostic engagement that sits below the Core subscription, as a way to qualify-and-convert without discounting the subscription itself?

Proposal: a two-week, €750 fixed-fee "Compound Diagnosis" before any plan commitment. Scope: one Onboarding Intake session + a Compounding Engine baseline pass (their Compound Score + top three momentum leaks) + a 60-minute read-out. Output: a 4–6 page diagnosis doc naming their score, biggest leaks, and recommended plan tier. Credit-back mechanic: if they sign a full plan within 30 days, the €750 is credited back as month-one credits (or waived) — removes the "I already paid" friction without cheapening the subscription. Why it works: solves a real objection ("how do I know this fits me before committing €3,500/mo?"), makes outbound far easier (€750 ask vs. €3,500/mo ask), and generates high-signal qualification. Ideal cohort-one mechanic. Watchout: don't let it drift into a standalone consulting product — the credit-back is what prevents that.

Open

Capacity plan — which post-cohort-one hire goes first?

The capacity plan supports a phased hire after cohort one clears. Which role comes first: a second operator, a designer, or a data/analytics lead? Each has a different effect on our Stream 1 margin and cohort ceiling.

Capability map · the strategic conversation

Three lanes of work, once the OS becomes a product.

Positioning GritFuel as a product — a Growth OS the client logs into — raises a capability question the original partnership shape didn't need to answer: who builds and operates the client-facing software? Rather than pre-assign partners, this map names the three lanes of work cleanly. Who leads which lane — and where lanes overlap — is a live conversation for the four of us, not a call to make in a deck.

In-scope capability

Automation lane

Campaign + internal process automation

  • Performance marketing delivery & optimisation
  • Campaign automation stack — paid media, creative ops, reporting pipelines
  • ALC Menu service automation (the Stream 2 margin driver)
  • Digitising GritFuel's internal processes — CRM, delivery workflows, ops dashboards

→ Powers Stream 2 margin as the ALC Menu automates. Enables scale without proportional headcount drift.

Shared interface

Integration zone

Where the two lanes meet

  • Data layer — Dashboard pulls from the automation + campaign stack
  • API integration between OS product and delivery tooling
  • Client delivery handoffs — Intake → OS → Playbook → Execution
  • Shared reporting & client-facing metrics

→ Weekly sync between lane leads on integration priorities. Shared roadmap, not a vendor relationship.

New capability · to discuss

Product-engineering lane

Client-facing OS product build

  • Compounding Dashboard — the client-facing hero
  • Onboarding Intake tool
  • Escalation & Handoff Router
  • Credit Allocation system
  • Compounding Engine — the scoring & momentum engine
  • Multi-tenant platform architecture & data model

→ Makes Stream 1 margin real. Without a dedicated owner here, the "OS" positioning and the subscription price point don't hold past the first cohort.

What this means for shareholding

Existing shareholdings stay where they are. Building a client-facing product is a new capability — not a contribution anyone owed. Equity for a first tech lead (industry norm: 2–3%, ideally drawn from a reserved 8–12% option pool for future product hires) either dilutes all shareholders proportionally or sits in a newly-created option pool. The strategic conversation we owe each other before 18 May: agreeing which partner leads which lane, sequencing the hire into the product-engineering lane, and letting Stream 1 revenue cover the salary from month one.

The shape of the hire — options we'd walk into this conversation with

Option A · Phased equity-for-work contractor. Senior full-stack at €5–7k/mo cash + 1–3% equity vesting over 24 months, funded from Stream 1 MRR (three signed clients at Core/Growth = ~€10–16k MRR covers it). Preferred shape. Option B · Straight revenue-funded contractor. €8–12k/mo, no equity — faster to find, retention risk, no founder stake. Option C · Dev shop + revenue-share. Fastest delivery, worst for long-term IP and product coherence. In parallel: WBSO (30–40% R&D tax credit) + MIT / Innovation Credit NL applications to take 20–35% off effective cost once in seat.
