One strategic conversation we owe each other

Positioning GritFuel as a Growth Operating System — a product the client logs into, not just an embedded service — surfaces a capability question the original partnership shape didn't need to answer. Three lanes of work — automation, integration, product-engineering — need naming; one of them (product-engineering) is a new capability outside the original scope. Who leads which lane is a live conversation, not a call to make in a deck. See the capability map on the Operating Model page for the full framing.

The premise

The buyer wants functionality. The market is big enough to build for it.

Two findings set the direction. The Founder / CEO DeepDive (persona work) landed a clear insight: our buyers value functionality above emotional resonance. They don't want to fall in love with their growth partner — they want a system that reliably does the job. That's the forcing function. It's what makes productising viable; if they wanted a relationship, a team-led model would still be the right answer. But functionality on its own doesn't make the monthly call. Someone has to read the Dashboard, pick the next play, and sign the Playbook — someone with context, a point of view, and skin in the outcome. That's the Strategist's role: not a growth partner to fall in love with, but the person who turns a reliable system into a reliable decision each month.

The TAM / SAM / SOM work supplied the second input. The post-seed segment in NL, wider EU, and South Africa sustains a seven-year path from seven clients to hundreds, and to roughly €38.8M ARR. That doesn't shape the product; it justifies building it at this level of ambition. Persona gave us the forcing function. Market sizing gave us the appetite.

THE BUYER · persona (Founder DeepDive) Wants functionality over resonance

Start-ups, scale-ups, and companies in transformation led by founders who are growth-led and ready to scale. They've been through agency churn and fractional CMO cycles. They don't want another relationship to manage — they want a system they can trust to run growth, so they can stop being the bottleneck. Functional reliability > warmth.

THE OPPORTUNITY · market sizing Big enough to justify the build

Post-seed start-ups, scale-ups, and companies in transformation in NL (€500k – €5m AR) + wider EU, plus SA (R5M – R100M AR). The SOM sustains a seven-year path to hundreds of clients and ~€38.8M ARR — headline numbers in the Market Sizing artefact. This is the perspective that justifies the ambition; it doesn't dictate the product's shape, but it makes the work worth doing.

Large · Underserved · Concentrated in our relationship networks

The capacity mismatch — and why productising is the only honest answer

Each Strategist holds a small number of client relationships — starting at around six, rising toward ten as the OS matures and automation carries more of the routine work. That's the binding constraint, and it's a deliberate one. The Strategist owns the judgment work (play selection, Playbook narrative, the monthly call on what matters), and that doesn't compress with effort. To serve the opportunity above at a team-led shape, we'd have to scale headcount linearly with revenue — which crushes margin, kills consistency, and drags the business back toward a consulting shape we explicitly don't want.

Honest trajectory: Year 1 stays at ~6 — productising doesn't magically escape the cap. What it does is let the cap rise over time. Year 2–3 ~10 / FTE as tool leverage shifts the strategist's hours from ~100% per client toward 60–70%, and partner elasticity on Stream 2 (.frank, Bluegyfu, BFL) means delivery scales outside our headcount. Year 4+ ~20+ / FTE as Phase 3 AI on the Compounding Engine and Escalation Router compresses the role from "six clients in parallel" to "supervise multiple systems in parallel." That's the only curve that connects seven clients today to hundreds at Y7 without collapsing under our own weight.

The response

The buyer wants a system. The market justifies building one. Team-led can't deliver both at the scale we need. Productising delivery — GritFuel OS, with a Compounding Growth Dashboard as the client-facing hero, a Monthly Playbook as the ritual, and a two-stream pricing model behind it — is the shape that lets the cap rise over time. Everything that follows is an expression of that single move.

The shape, in one page

Growth OS, with a Compounding Dashboard at the centre.

GritFuel is a Growth OS with two halves that work together. An embedded team as the engine (the senior operators who run a client's growth month-to-month), and a product as the interface (the Dashboard a founder sees, the Playbook they read, the tools that keep the rhythm consistent). Same thesis, with a sharper shape than a pure service can carry.

The JTBD / Value Proposition Canvas work is what grounds that shape in the buyer. Walking founders through their jobs, pains, and gains makes two things obvious: (1) what they actually want is a system they can point at, not a senior operator they rent; and (2) the emotional payoff they're chasing — "our growth is compounding, and I can see it" — lives in a live view, not in a monthly document. Those two findings shape the product, the hero artefact, and the pricing logic in that order.

THE ENGINE Embedded growth team

Senior operators running the monthly rhythm for each client. Tagline that carries this: Human thinking. Relentless execution. The team is the judgement layer — play selection, Playbook narrative, client relationship. This stays, and scales via tools, not headcount.

Three moves that hold the shape together

How the premise becomes a product.

The premise (functionality-first buyer + scale-justifying market + capped team-led shape) points in one direction. The three moves below are how it becomes a product. Each move is grounded in a specific exercise, not a preference. Reading them in order shows why the pieces hang together the way they do, and where a partner challenge would actually land.

Step 0 · Premise · Buyers want functionality over resonance

Exercise that grounds it: Founder / CEO DeepDive (persona work). Our buyers value functional reliability above emotional warmth — they want a system they can trust to run growth, not a relationship to manage. That's the finding that makes productising viable at all; without it, a team-led shape would remain the right answer. TAM / SAM / SOM sizing supplies the second input — perspective on the scale of the prize (hundreds of clients, ~€38.8M ARR over seven years) — appetite, not direction. The combination (functionality-first buyer + scale-justifying market + capped team-led shape) is what shapes the three moves that follow.

Premise

Move 1 · Embedded team + Growth OS, together

Exercise that grounds it: JTBD / Value Proposition Canvas (Strategy). With the premise pointing at a productised shape, the buyer work checks whether they'd actually want one. Founders don't articulate their job as "find me a senior operator" — they articulate it as "give me a system I trust to run growth, so I can stop being the bottleneck." That frames the product as an operating system with an embedded team as the engine behind it — not one or the other.

Step 1

Move 2 · Compounding Growth Dashboard as the client-facing hero

Exercise that grounds it: the Gain Creators column of the VPC, then pressure-tested against the Founder DeepDive. The gain founders name most often — and the thing they'd pay to keep feeling — is compounding visibility: the sense that growth is stacking and they can see it. A monthly document doesn't produce that feeling; an always-on view does. The Playbook is essential as the monthly ritual, and sits behind the Dashboard as the ritual artefact.

Step 2

Move 3 · Two revenue streams, one invoice

Exercise that grounds it: the Fixed-Cost Model + ALC Menu work (Operating Model). Once the product is a system rather than a team, the economics split naturally: pricing access to the system (Stream 1 · fixed subscription, SaaS-like margin) is a different motion from pricing the work done inside it (Stream 2 · credit pool on the ALC Menu, 75% cost-share to delivery partners). Collapsing them back into one number reads like an agency retainer, which is exactly what GritFuel isn't.

Step 3

What this means for the rest of the briefing

Every section downstream — Product, Brand, Operating Model, GTM — is the expression of one of these three moves. If a move is wrong, the downstream work inherits that wrongness. So if you disagree with any of them, flag it here first, before you read the section that builds on it. That's the fastest way to keep us aligned before soft launch.
