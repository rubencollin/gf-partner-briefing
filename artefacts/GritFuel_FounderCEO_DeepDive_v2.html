F/C

"Lars" — Founder / CEO

Series A · 25 employees · 18 months runway · EU tech SME
Raised €3M, has an MVP with early customers, board wants a credible growth function before the next round. Cannot yet justify a €150k/yr CMO.

Functional Jobs

What they're trying to get done
  • Convert MVP traction into repeatable, defensible market share
  • Hit monthly & quarterly growth milestones the board tracks
  • Extend runway by growing revenue faster than burn
  • Build a growth function without hiring in-house marketing
  • Know per-euro what is working and what to cut

Emotional Jobs

How they want to feel
  • In control of growth — not hostage to an agency's creative director
  • Confident presenting traction to board & next-round investors
  • Relieved that burn-rate vs. traction anxiety has a rhythm
  • Credible as a CEO who "runs a real company"

Social Jobs

How they want to be perceived
  • Seen as a founder who "gets" modern growth
  • Respected by peer CEOs for disciplined, data-led scaling
  • Avoid the silent shame of "we couldn't make it work"
Customer Profile
What Lars carries into the room
Pains

  • Fractional CMO ceiling — strategy deck, nothing to execute it with.

  • Board-ready narrative: "here's spend, return, and next month"
  • Headcount-light, variable-cost growth function that scales with revenue
  • Senior thinking without a €180k/yr CMO hire

What this tells us

The top five opportunity jobs are all functional or anxiety-reducing. Not social, not aspirational. Lars is not buying to look sophisticated to peers — he's buying to stop the anxiety of not knowing whether his burn rate is buying him a business or just more months. GritFuel's sales motion should lead with the measurable (Compound Score, attribution, runway math). The emotional payoff lands as a consequence of the evidence, not the pitch. If the first slide of the sales deck is about "a new category" rather than "where is each euro going," we're solving our own problem, not Lars's.

03
Trigger Events
Founders grumble about marketing every day. They only shop when something specific happens. These are the nine most likely triggers — GritFuel's outreach, content, and sales motion should be designed to intercept them.
Board / Investor
Bad board meeting

Growth slide was weak. An investor made a pointed comment about CAC or pipeline. Founder walked out humiliated.

Signal to watch: board meetings quarterly — outreach timed T+3 days. Content that uses "that slide" or "the growth slide" as a hook.
Commercial
Fundraise kick-off

Seed → Series A or A → B. Founder needs a defensible growth story, clean attribution, and six months of evidence before pitch meetings.

Signal to watch: new raises announced, new investor dev roles hired. 6-month prep window is the sales cycle.
Financial
CFO finds a spend anomaly

Quarterly review. CFO can't reconcile marketing spend to outcomes. Founder now has to explain attribution under pressure.

Signal to watch: CFO job posts, "finance meets marketing" content resonance, Q1/Q3 fiscal cycle moments.
People
Marketing lead quits or is let go

Suddenly no one owns growth. Founder needs continuity without a 3-month hiring cycle. Vendor relationships become untethered.

Signal to watch: LinkedIn "open to work" from ex-marketing leads, departure announcements.
Vendor
Agency contract renewal

90/60/30 day window. Founder re-evaluates. This is the moment they're most open to a structurally different option.

Signal to watch: contract cycles — usually Jan / July for calendar-year contracts. Targeted outreach 60 days out.
Competitive
Competitor raises / launches / wins

Founder sees a competitor get traction and panics. Pressure on their own team doubles overnight.

Signal to watch: PR announcements in the target's category. Contact within 2 weeks of competitor news.
Retention
Churn spike or metric miss

A quarter where growth was meant to accelerate but flatlined. Realisation that the growth engine is brittle, not structural.

Signal to watch: earnings/retention dips in small-cap public peers; private signals via LinkedIn / layoff news.
Product
New feature / market launch

Product ships something that needs GTM, but current team can't ship the messaging, positioning, and paid activation inside 6 weeks.

Signal to watch: product roadmap hints, beta waitlists. Slower sale cycle (90–120 days) but higher intent.
Strategy
Fractional CMO departure

Strategy vacuum. Founder realises they paid for decks, not deliverables, and needs a structurally different model next time.

Signal to watch: ex-fractional-CMO announcements, "we're rethinking marketing leadership" language in founder content.
Operational implication

The three hot triggers (bad board meeting, fundraise kick-off, CFO spend anomaly) all share a common shape: someone senior just asked a question about growth the founder couldn't answer cleanly. That means GritFuel's single sharpest outreach hook is not "we do marketing better" — it's "the next time someone asks you where your growth euros went, here's how you answer in 60 seconds". Build outbound, content, and the first sales conversation around that scenario.

04
Objection Map
The ten things you'll hear in the first fifty sales conversations, with sharpened handling for each. These should live on a one-page internal battlecard and be rehearsed before any external pitch.
01
We already work with an agency
Handle: "Perfect — that's exactly who we replace. Quick question: what's the last thing your agency shipped that actually moved your growth number?" Then listen. 90% of answers are about activity, not outcome. Bridge to Compound Score.
02
€3,500/mo is too expensive for our stage
Handle: Reframe: we're 70% of EU benchmarks, paid media at cost (no markup), and one contract replaces 3–5 vendor relationships. Total-cost-of-ownership, not sticker price. If the answer is still "too expensive," they're not at the target stage — disqualify kindly.
03
Credits sound complicated
Handle: "1 credit = €25 of output value. Your tier gives you 70/110/180 credits a month. Here's last month's Playbook for a real client — credits applied line by line." Show, don't explain. If they still push back, credits may actually be complicated — a signal, not a win.
04
How is this different from a fractional CMO?
Handle: A fractional CMO is one brain, no delivery arms. GritFuel is brain + arms + memory. The Compounding Engine is what no single person (however senior) can give you — institutional memory of your own growth, monthly.
05
We'd rather build in-house
Handle: "In-house is the right end-state. €300k+/yr, 6-month hiring cycle, and a risk that your first hire isn't senior enough to matter. GritFuel is the bridge: senior thinking from day one at ~€55k/yr — and when you're ready to hire, we hand over the Playbook, data, and OS to your in-house team."
06
How do I know the execution is actually good?
Handle: Month-to-month contract. Cancel anytime. Credits roll over 3 months. You see every deliverable and every data point inside the Dashboard. For beta, we also offer named calls with our existing beta cohort. If they want a fixed contract — they're not our ICP.
07
My Head of Growth won't want this
Handle: "Good — they shouldn't, if we're badly positioned. GritFuel is their co-pilot, not their replacement. Let's get them in the room and I'll show them how this makes them look senior." Offer the HoG a separate 30-minute session focused on their objections.
08
What if our strategist leaves?
Handle: Strategist rotation is planned — every client hears from a second strategist at quarterly review anyway. The OS holds the institutional memory, not the individual. Show the Onboarding Intake + Playbook stack as the receipt of that memory.
09
Why haven't we heard of you?
Handle: "Because we're in beta and we want it that way. First 10 clients get founder pricing, direct founder access, and first dibs on feature requests. By the time you've heard of us through a cold ad, the preferential rates are gone." Scarcity works when it's structurally true.
10
Can you guarantee results?
Handle: "No responsible growth partner ever should. What we guarantee is honesty: every month your Compound Score tells you whether we're working — and at any point, you cancel. That's a stronger guarantee than a contract nobody can actually enforce."
05
Two Buyer Archetypes
Within the Founder/CEO segment, two distinct archetypes buy for opposite reasons. Same OS underneath — different products in how they're sold. Getting this wrong = lost deal + burned referral.
A

The Anxious Founder

First-time · Growth stalled · Board stress
Psychological state

Fear-driven. Growth has plateaued. Board meetings feel adversarial. Every decision is filtered through "what if this doesn't work — who's to blame?" Wants reassurance more than optimisation.

Dominant jobs
  • Reduce burn-vs-traction anxiety (#5 opportunity)
  • Extend runway by growing revenue faster than burn (#2)
  • Avoid the silent shame of "we couldn't make it work" (#9)
What they actually want

Defensibility + someone to share the risk with. They want a partner they can point to if growth slows — not one they can blame afterward, but one who's on the hook with them in real time.

Watch-outs
  • Concentration of accountability is a bug, not a feature, for anxious buyers
  • Will churn on the first bad month unless we pre-brief the expected shape
  • Decision-making gets overridden by board anxiety — secure the board early
Sales motion
  • Lead with case studies + references, even prototype ones
  • Emphasise cancel-anytime, month-to-month, credit rollover
  • Offer the first 30 days as a "diagnostic month" — lower-risk on-ramp
  • Include the board / investor in the pitch where possible
  • Language: process, predictability, "we've seen this 100 times"
  • Avoid: "bold," "new category," "disruptive"
C

The Confident Founder

Repeat · Prior exit · Strong conviction
Psychological state

Leverage-driven. Growth isn't existential — it's a resource-allocation problem. Treats marketing as a tool to deploy, not a question to solve. Wants speed and sophistication, not hand-holding.

Dominant jobs
  • Convert MVP traction into repeatable market share (#1)
  • Know per-euro what's working and what to cut (#3)
  • Build growth function without in-house marketing team (#8)
What they actually want

Outcomes + independence. They don't want to sit in your process — they want to see the OS do the work. Data, levers, a dashboard they can check at 11pm. "Don't drown me in meetings."

Watch-outs
  • Will push for customisation — stay firm on the OS as the product
  • Will negotiate on pricing — the beta-pricing scarcity line matters here
  • May bypass strategist altogether — set explicit cadence in the contract
  • High-leverage referral source, IF you deliver. Worth the extra effort.
Sales motion
  • Lead with peer-CEO intros, founder-to-founder selling
  • Short pitch, hard data, short deck — respect the time
  • Emphasise Compound Score, attribution depth, OS mechanics
  • Only involve the board if the founder requests it
  • Language: compound returns, speed, built-by-operators, leverage
  • Avoid: "process," "journey," "discovery call"

Stress Test — v2
Sharpened assumptions list. The partner-risk concern from v1 is now retired. Four live structural assumptions remain — these are the things most likely to break when prospects show up.
Retracted since v1: "Partners will stay invisible and cooperative at 75% cost share." This was flagged as the biggest structural risk in v1. It does not apply — .frank, BFL, and Bluegyfu share common ownership with GritFuel, making the core delivery layer structurally stable, not a fragile partnership pyramid. Outer creative-layer freelancers remain third-party but lack the capability and credibility to compete at the core-service layer.

Live weakest assumptions

The four most likely to break
1 Founders will pay for a system they can't see.
The OS is 5 tools — but tools aren't emotional; growth is. The Compound Score and Dashboard need to be the emotional hook, not just the back-office. Right now they're described as outputs — they need to be the product.
2 SMEs with €50–500k budgets want a system, not a relationship.
EU mid-market buying behavior is deeply relationship-driven. The OS is the rational argument; the human/trust layer closes. Do target founders want a "system" — or Ruben + founders, with the system as proof they'll deliver?
3 The credit model reduces friction.
Could equally add cognitive load. Every budget-minded client will weigh credits as abstraction-disguised-as-transparency. Validate with actual prospects whether "1 credit = €25 output" clarifies or confuses.
4 No agency can copy this in the 18-month head-start window.
S4 Capital, WPP SME arms, Dept — any of them could copy the OS model in 12–18 months once validated. The data-accumulation moat is real but slow (6–12 months per client). Defensibility is mostly head start, not inherent. Sequence of client acquisition matters.

Fit gaps — the specific fixes

Pain relievers that don't yet land
→ Compound Score must become the product's face
Lead client communication with it. Email it monthly. "Your Compound Score is 73 — up from 68." Dashboard hero, not a footer. If the founder can brag with it, we've won.
→ Spend-to-result pipeline needs visualisation
€X → credits → output → result should be one flow in the Playbook and Dashboard. Currently implied, not expressed. A Sankey or waterfall view closes the attribution-claim gap.
→ Precise language on attribution
Dashboards show activity; attribution shows causation. Be explicit about the level of attribution promised (channel-level, MMM-lite, deterministic-with-gaps). Over-claim once and you churn.
→ Senior-strategist brand must be visible
Prospects can't tell if they're getting a senior operator or a junior account lead. Monthly founder POV content (LinkedIn, podcast, newsletter) is the cheapest way to pre-sell senior quality before the call.
→ Migration-away-from-incumbent playbook
Many targets are 12–24 months into a 36-month agency contract. Leaving hurts. Offer an explicit 60-day transition plan — run parallel during wind-down — so switching cost is a product, not a prospect problem.

Five questions for the first ten prospect conversations

These will validate or break this whole canvas faster than any desk research
  1. When was the last time you changed marketing vendor — and what, specifically, triggered the switch?
  2. If we took GritFuel away from you tomorrow, what would you fall back to? Walk me through day one.
  3. What would you miss first: the strategy, the execution, or the data?
  4. Which month were you closest to cancelling? What saved it?
  5. Who have you told about us — and what exactly did you say?
