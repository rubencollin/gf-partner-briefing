Section 02 · Product

From a monthly promise to a monthly product.

The public site today sells a Monthly Growth System delivered by an embedded team. The sprint turned that promise into a repeatable product: five tools, a playbook template, and an operating rhythm anyone on the team can run.

The shape

A promise, and the machinery behind it.

GritFuel has two expressions of the same thing. The Monthly Growth System is the promise we make to a founder — the language they meet on the site and in a pitch. GritFuel OS is the machinery that keeps the promise — five tools + a Playbook template that run the same operating rhythm for every client, so delivery doesn't rely on heroics from any one operator.

THE PROMISE Monthly Growth System

The founder-facing framework. Four-step rhythm: PLUG IN → ALIGN → EXECUTE → SCALE. Output is a custom monthly plan per client, anchored in the Monthly Growth System logic. This is the IP and the language.

Promise-shaped · Founder-facing · The language of the product

THE MACHINERY GritFuel OS

The first Growth Operating System. Five productised tools + a Monthly Playbook template. Every client runs the same rhythm — intake, compounding, escalation, credit allocation, dashboarding — with their own data. This is how we keep the promise at scale.

Product-shaped · Delivered repeatably · Frees operator time for judgement

Why this matters

We need both. One is the language of our promise to the founder; the other is the machinery of how we keep it. The open call is which one leads on the public site, and which one sits behind it — a decision in the block below.

A system, and the human who runs it

The OS is what keeps the work repeatable. A dedicated Strategist is what keeps it reliable — the person who interprets the Dashboard each month, chooses the next play, writes the Playbook narrative, and stays accountable to the outcome. Functionality doesn't do judgment; the Strategist is where judgment lives. Human thinking, relentless execution — the system is the second half; the Strategist is the first.

The operating rhythm

Five tools, one monthly loop.

Each tool owns a step in the monthly cycle. Intake captures the founder's reality; the Growth Dashboard scores where we are; the Compounding Engine picks the next play; the Escalation Router handles exceptions; Credit Allocation sets the spend envelope. The Monthly Playbook is the artefact the founder signs off at the start of each cycle — the plan the five tools hand to them, and the commitment the next month runs against.

Key decisions

What's locked, what's open.

Five-tool architecture — locked

Intake, Dashboard, Compounding, Escalation, Credit Allocation. Each has a defined input, output, and owner. Everything else is a feature on top of these five.

Locked

Client-facing hero: Monthly Playbook, Compounding Growth Dashboard, or both?

Two candidates for the #1 artefact a founder sees. Playbook: the monthly deliverable — plan, decisions, experiments, next steps. Narrative, warm, ritualistic. Dashboard: the always-on view of compounding growth — the tool we actually talk about as the selling point. Live, honest, always there. If the Dashboard is the #1 selling point, then "Playbook-as-hero" is wrong — we'd lead demos, the site, and onboarding with the Dashboard and use the Playbook as the monthly ritual behind it. The third option is both, with clearly separated roles. Needs the four of us.

Open

How much of the tooling do we expose to clients?

Founders will ask. Options: (a) keep tools internal, deliver only the Playbook; (b) expose the Dashboard only; (c) give full portal access. Each has pricing and ops implications.

Open

Does the public site lead with "GritFuel OS" or keep "Monthly Growth System"?

"GritFuel OS — the first Growth Operating System" is sharper and more differentiated, but further from the category language founders already know. "Monthly Growth System" is warmer and on-brand with the current site.

Open

Dashboard metrics — controllables, outcomes, or both layered?

Real product-design question. Outcomes the client cares about (revenue growth, customer count, retention) aren't ours to move directly; controllables we drive (pipeline velocity, lead quality, channel mix, content-to-lead ratio) are. If the Dashboard shows only outcomes, we're taking credit for things outside the system's control. If it shows only controllables, we're measuring activity, not value — exactly the anti-pattern founders are escaping.

Proposal: three layers, wired together. (1) Outcome layer — revenue / customer / retention, shown as context, not as our claim. (2) Leading-indicator layer — what we actually move, framed as the bets we're willing to be measured on. (3) Compound Score — 0–100 hero number rolling the leading indicators via the four weighted signals (channel momentum 30% · goal proximity 25% · credit efficiency 25% · dependency sequencing 20%). Plus a connection layer drawing the explicit line from leading indicators to outcomes with the historical lag. That's the honest pitch: "we don't drive revenue — we drive the compounding leading indicators that, over 60–90 days, tend to."

Open

Escalation Router — human-in-the-loop or automated first?

The spec supports both. Starting automated is faster and cheaper; starting with a human-in-loop gives us better training data. Preference?

Capability · one clean conversation

Where the OS-as-product sits in the partnership.

Every artefact on this page describes the OS as software. Today most of it is HTML prototypes, spec documents, and working spreadsheets — a well-thought-out draft, not production software a client logs into. Closing that gap is a product-engineering capability that wasn't named in the original partnership scope. The client-facing OS product itself is a new shape of work; who leads it is a conversation, not a pre-assigned role.

Build order, once a product-engineering lead is in seat

  1. Harden the OS as described above — Onboarding Intake, Growth Dashboard, Compounding Engine, Escalation Router, Credit Allocation — to a production standard. This is the "first Growth Operating System" claim, made real.
  2. Automate the ALC Menu — turn the services catalog from a human-dispatched list into software-assisted delivery. This is where Stream 2 moves from 75% cost-share to actual productised margin. Sits naturally in the automation lane.

The full framing — automation lane · integration zone · product-engineering lane, plus how equity for a tech lead would be structured — lives on the capability map in the Operating Model section. See also Risks and Decisions.

What I need from you

Three specific questions on Product.

  1. Client-facing hero: is the Compounding Growth Dashboard the #1 artefact a founder sees (and the one we demo / lead the site with), with the Monthly Playbook as the ritual behind it — or is the Playbook the hero with the Dashboard narrative-only? This is upstream of tool exposure.
  2. Language on the public site: does "GritFuel OS — the first Growth Operating System" lead, or does "Monthly Growth System" stay as the front door with GritFuel OS sitting behind it as the engine?
  3. Escalation Router start state: human-in-the-loop for the first cohort (slower, better data) or automated from day one (faster, rougher)?
